\section*{Introduction}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\textcolor{blue}{Diabetic peripheral neuropathy (DPN) is the most common type of polyneuropathy affecting 42-
58\% of Filipinos \cite{ropper2019adams, araneta2006hyperglycemia, dagang2016validation}.
}
Characterized by progressive nerve damage, DPN often presents with symptoms such as pain, numbness, and loss of sensation in the extremities, particularly the feet. Beyond its direct impact on quality of life, DPN is a major risk factor for foot ulcers, infections, and lower-limb amputations, contributing significantly to healthcare burden.

\textcolor{blue}{Despite being associated with reduced quality of life and debilitating complications,
DPN is underdiagnosed in up to 81\% of patients \cite{vinik2012neuropathy, ziegler2018painful}. Hurdles in the early diagnosis of DPN includes the insidious progression, inconsistent disease presentation and the need for complicated equipment for definitive diagnosis \cite{vinik2012neuropathy, ziegler2018painful, casellini2013sudoscan}.
}

May be good to include a sentence on Nerve Conduction Studies>} \textcolor{blue}{Skin biopsy with quantitation of intraepidermal nerve fiber density is still the gold standard in detecting small fiber neuropathy, while sudorimetry is a rapidly evolving technology that can easily be integrated into clinical practice. The development of such technologies like the Sudoscan allows noninvasive and easy-to-perform tests for detection of small fiber neuropathy based on reverse iontophoresis and chronoamperometry to measure sudomotor function \cite{mayaudon:2010newsimple, vinik2015newage}.
}

Early detection and management of DPN are therefore critical, yet remain challenging due to the multifactorial nature of the disease and the heterogeneity of patient profiles. In recent years, machine learning (ML) has emerged as a powerful tool for modeling complex, nonlinear relationships in medical data. By leveraging large clinical datasets, ML algorithms can identify patterns and interactions among risk factors that may not be readily apparent through traditional statistical approaches. 

Numerous studies have developed ML models with good discriminatory performance (AUCs ranging from 0.64 to 0.93), employing diverse algorithms such as random forest, XGBoost, and gradient boosting. Models often incorporate clinical, biochemical, and even traditional Chinese medicine features, enhancing predictive accuracy and relevance \cite{ma2025machine,jiang2024multifeature,cui2023study,lian2023Study,Wei2024construction}. Internal validation methods like cross-validation are widely used, and some studies report calibration and decision curve analyses, supporting clinical utility \cite{cui2023study}.

Despite the  predictive performance, many ML models—particularly ensemble and deep learning methods—are often criticized as “black boxes,” limiting their adoption in clinical settings where interpretability and trust are essential. To address this limitation, the field of explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) has gained increasing attention. XAI techniques aim to make model predictions more transparent by providing insights into how input features influence outcomes. Methods such as feature importance scores, SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations), and partial dependence plots have been widely used to offer global and local interpretability. 

The application of XAI techniques such as SHAP, LIME, and Anchor explanations is increasingly common, providing transparent insights into feature importance and model decisions, which is critical for clinical trust and adoption \cite{wu2025prognostic,jiang2024multifeature, pandey2024decoding, wu2024interpretable, sheikh2025predicting}. Integration of XAI with multimodal data enables identification of key risk factors and supports personalized treatment recommendations \cite{nguyen2024interpretable, alHudaibi2025explainable}. Some frameworks incorporate human-in-the-loop feedback to enhance interpretability and clinical relevance \cite{lalwani2025generative}. While these approaches help elucidate model behavior, they primarily describe associations rather than actionable insights, which are crucial for clinical decision-making.

Counterfactual explanations represent a more recent and clinically meaningful approach within XAI. Instead of merely explaining why a prediction was made, counterfactuals answer “what-if” questions by identifying minimal changes to a patient’s features that would lead to a different predicted outcome. For example, a counterfactual explanation might suggest how modifying certain clinical variables—such as glycemic control or body mass index—could reduce a patient’s predicted risk of developing DPN. This aligns closely with the needs of personalized medicine, where interventions are tailored to individual patients based on modifiable risk factors. Several methodological approaches for generating counterfactual explanations have been proposed in the literature. One of the most popular is the DiCE framework \cite{mothilal2020explaining}, an optimization-based method, which formulate counterfactual generation as a constrained optimization problem that seeks minimal changes to input features while achieving a desired prediction;

The integration of machine learning and counterfactual explanations offers a promising framework for both the prediction and interpretation of diabetic peripheral neuropathy. By combining high predictive accuracy with actionable, patient-specific insights, this approach has the potential to support clinicians in early diagnosis, risk assessment, and treatment planning. Moreover, it bridges the gap between advanced computational models and practical clinical utility, fostering greater trust and adoption of AI-driven tools in healthcare. 

This study aims to investigate the application of machine learning models in conjunction with counterfactual explanation techniques for the prediction and interpretation of diabetic peripheral neuropathy (DPN) risk, thereby contributing to the growing body of research on interpretable and actionable artificial intelligence in medicine. The study also examines the potential insights derived from incorporating nerve conduction study results and Sudoscan measurements—modalities that are comparatively underrepresented in many AI-based DPN studies, which often rely primarily on routine clinical and demographic variables. Furthermore, by utilizing data collected within the Philippine clinical context, this work enhances the geographic diversity of current studies and provides context-specific insights into the application of explainable AI for DPN.

\subsection*{Data Collection}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\textcolor{blue}{
A total of 201 adult Filipino patients diagnosed with Diabetes Mellitus (DM) were recruited
between 2022 and 2023. Subjects with limb amputation, gangrenous/ necrotic foot, with implanted
cardiac pacemakers or defibrillators, and patients with other known causes of polyneuropathy were
excluded. Remaining 190 qualified subjects underwent a series of detailed assessment comprising of
Michigan Neuropathy Screening Instrument (MNSI) test, serial neurologic tests, and lower extremity NCV
examination to detect the presence of DPN. Subjects were then classified based on the 2009 Toronto
consensus criteria as confirmed, probable, possible or no DPN \cite{tesfaye2010diabetic}. This study was approved by the local Institutional Ethics Review Board and all subjects gave their written consent prior to participating.
}

\textcolor{blue}{
Neuropathic symptoms were documented by subjectively asking patients for presence of any
distal extremity numbness, paresthesia, or pain, and by asking them to answer the Filipino validated
version of MNSI \cite{dagang2016validation}. Abnormal neurologic signs were detected using the standard 10g Semmes Weinstein monofilament examination (SWME) and standard 128-Hz clanging tuning fork tests \cite{feng2009semmes,oyer2007quantitative}. Additional examination for pinprick sensation was done using the same criteria for SWME. Achilles tendon reflex testing was also done and dichotomized subjects as either normal or hypo/areflexive. All neurological examinations were conducted by a single trained physician.
}

\textcolor{blue}{
Nerve conduction studies were performed at a stable skin temperature of 32°C and a room
temperature of 24°C using Nihon Kohen EMG equipment with a filter width of 20 Hz to 3 kHz, amplifier
setting at 200 $\mu$V/div, and sweep speed of 10 ms/div. 
}

\textcolor{blue}{
The following nerve attributes were measured: 
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0em 
\item Sural sensory nerve action potentials (SNAP) and conduction velocities (CV)
\item Superficial peroneal SNAP and CV
\item Tibial motor nerve compound muscle action potentials (CMAP), CV, distal latencies (DL) and
F-wave latencies
\end{itemize}
}

\textcolor{blue}{
All NCV testing was done by a single trained technician on the same day as other
neurological examinations. Finally, sudomotor testing using Sudoscan was performed on each participant. Subjects placed palms of their hands and the soles of their feet on stainless steel electrodes and an incremental low direct voltage (<4V) was applied for about 2 minutes. The device produced an average electrochemical skin conductance (ESC) result for both hands and feet which is based on stimulation of sweat glands by the low-level voltage, allowing evidence of sweat dysfunction \cite{garcia2022detection}.
}

\section*{Methodology}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
This section describes the machine learning model development pipeline and the counterfactual generation methodology.

\subsection*{Machine Learning Modeling}
In this section, we describe the machine learning models used, stratified cross-validation, feature selection and hyperparameter optimization.

\subsubsection*{Models}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

A diverse set of machine learning algorithms was evaluated in this study using default or standard parameter settings to ensure a consistent and unbiased baseline comparison. These models can be broadly grouped according to their underlying learning paradigms:

\textbf{(a) Linear and Probabilistic Models}.
This group includes Logistic Regression, Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA), Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGDClassifier), Naive Bayes (GaussianNB), and Linear Support Vector Machine (Linear SVM). These models are generally characterized by strong interpretability and computational efficiency. Logistic Regression and Linear SVM are effective in high-dimensional spaces and provide robust linear decision boundaries. LDA and QDA model class-conditional distributions, with QDA allowing nonlinear decision boundaries through class-specific covariance matrices. Naive Bayes is particularly efficient and performs well under conditional independence assumptions, while SGDClassifier enables scalable optimization for large datasets.

\textbf{(b) Distance-Based and Instance-Based Learning}.
The k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) algorithm falls under this category. It is a non-parametric method that makes predictions based on the proximity of data points in feature space. Its strength lies in its simplicity and ability to capture complex decision boundaries without explicit model training, although it may be sensitive to feature scaling and noise.

\textbf{(c) Tree-Based and Ensemble Methods}.
This group includes Decision Tree, Random Forest, Extra Trees, and Gradient Boosting. Decision Trees are intuitive and capable of capturing nonlinear relationships and feature interactions. Random Forest and Extra Trees extend this capability through ensemble learning, improving generalization and reducing variance via bagging and randomized feature selection. Gradient Boosting builds models sequentially to correct previous errors, often achieving strong predictive performance.

\textbf{(d) Advanced Gradient Boosting Frameworks}.
More sophisticated implementations of boosting are represented by XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost. These algorithms are optimized for efficiency and performance on structured/tabular data. XGBoost incorporates regularization and efficient handling of sparse data; LightGBM employs histogram-based learning and leaf-wise tree growth for speed and scalability; CatBoost is particularly effective in handling categorical variables and mitigating overfitting through ordered boosting techniques.

\textbf{(e) Kernel-Based Models}.
The RBF Support Vector Machine (RBF SVM) extends linear SVM by applying a radial basis function kernel, enabling the modeling of nonlinear decision boundaries. This makes it powerful for complex datasets where linear separability is not sufficient.

\textbf{(f) Baseline Model}.
A Naive classifier that predicts the majority class was included as a baseline to contextualize the performance of all other models.

Overall, this diverse selection of models allows for a comprehensive evaluation across linear, nonlinear, probabilistic, and ensemble-based learning paradigms, each with distinct strengths in terms of interpretability, flexibility, robustness, and predictive power.

\subsubsection*{Repeated Stratified k-Fold Cross-Validation}

Given the relatively limited number of unconfirmed cases in the dataset, a robust evaluation strategy was used to generate reliable performance estimates. A repeated stratified k-fold cross-validation scheme was employed, with 4 folds and 10 repetitions, resulting in a total of 40 validation runs per model.

Stratification ensures that each fold preserves the class distribution of the dataset, which is particularly important in imbalanced classification settings. Repetition further reduces variance in performance estimates by averaging results across multiple random partitions.

\subsubsection*{Performance Metrics}
For each validation run, key performance metrics were recorded, including Receiver Operating Characteristic Area Under the Curve (ROC-AUC), Youden Index, Sensitivity (Recall), and Specificity. These metrics were selected to provide a balanced evaluation of discriminative ability and clinical relevance, particularly in distinguishing between confirmed and unconfirmed cases.

\subsubsection*{Feature Selection}

To assess the contribution of different feature subsets, a structured feature selection and ablation strategy was implemented.

First, all models were trained using the complete set of available features to establish baseline performance. Subsequently, based on the feature groupings defined in Table 1, models were retrained while systematically removing each feature group. This ablation approach enables the identification of feature groups that contribute most significantly to predictive performance.

In addition, multicollinearity was addressed using the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). Features exhibiting a VIF greater than 5 were considered highly collinear and were removed, resulting in a reduced feature set aimed at improving model stability and interpretability.

To further investigate the predictive value of specific diagnostic modalities, models were also trained exclusively on nerve conduction study (NCS) features and Sudoscan features, respectively. This allows for a focused evaluation of their standalone predictive capabilities and their potential role in clinical decision support.

\subsubsection*{Hyperparameter Optimization}
Following the comparative evaluation, the best-performing model was selected based on the results of the previous steps. In this study, CatBoost demonstrated superior performance and was therefore chosen for further optimization, as detailed in the Results and Discussion section.

Hyperparameter optimization was conducted using a Repeated Stratified k-Fold framework with 3 splits. For each split, the training portion of the data was subjected to a further 3-fold Bayesian optimization, with ROC-AUC as the objective function. This approach efficiently explores the hyperparameter space by balancing exploration and exploitation, leading to improved model configurations.

The optimal hyperparameters obtained from each training fold were then used to generate predictions on the corresponding test fold. A classification threshold was subsequently selected for each split, allowing for the calibration of sensitivity and specificity in accordance with the study objectives.

The mean and standard deviations of the threshold, ROC-AUC, Youden index, specificity and sensitivity are reported for the 3 splits, as well as the confidence interval at 5\% for thr ROC-AUC and the Youden index.

This multi-stage optimization framework ensures that the final model is both well-tuned and rigorously validated, enhancing its reliability and generalizability.

\subsection*{Counterfactual Generation}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

To provide interpretable insights into the predictions of the trained machine learning models, counterfactual explanations were generated using the DiCE (Diverse Counterfactual Explanations) framework \cite{mothilal2020explaining}. DiCE is well-suited for this study due to its model-agnostic capability, enabling its application across a wide range of classifiers, including complex ensemble models. Furthermore, it supports diversity-aware counterfactual generation, allowing the identification of multiple plausible feature modifications that can alter model predictions—an important requirement in clinical decision support. Its ability to incorporate feature constraints (e.g., permitted ranges and immutable variables) ensures that generated counterfactuals remain realistic and clinically meaningful.

\subsubsection*{General Setup}

\paragraph{Global Permitted Range}
To ensure feasibility of generated counterfactuals, a global permitted range was defined for all continuous features. The bounds were computed as follow:
\begin{itemize}
    \itemsep0em 
    \item Minimum: $min(0,x_{min} - \sigma)$
    \item Maximum: $x_{max} + \sigma)$
\end{itemize}
where $x_{min}$ and $x_{max}$ denote the observed minimum and maximum values respectively of each feature for the entire dataset, while $\sigma)$ is the standard deviation. This formulation prevents unrealistic values while allowing controlled extrapolation beyond the observed data.

Local counterfactual explanations were generated from borderline and misclassified cases. Borderline cases are for instances whose predicted probabilities lie within ±0.2 of the mean decision threshold obtained during hyperparameter optimization. These instances represent cases where small feature changes may alter the predicted outcome, making them particularly informative for analysis.

\paragraph{Local Permitted Range}
For each selected instance, an instance-specific permitted range was defined for each feature. Similar to the global permitted range, this ensures that generated counterfactuals remain both realistic and tailored to the specific instance.
